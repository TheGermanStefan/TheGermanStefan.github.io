🔵 Coordinating conjunctions — word order UNCHANGED

The conjunctions und, aber, oder, denn, sondern join two main clauses. The verb stays in position 2 in both clauses — nothing changes.

ConjunctionMeaningWord order effectExample
undandV2 maintainedIch lerne Deutsch und ich spreche es gut.
aberbutV2 maintainedEr ist müde, aber er arbeitet weiter.
oderorV2 maintainedKommst du heute oder kommst du morgen?
dennbecause (coord.)V2 maintained ⚠️Er bleibt zu Hause, denn er ist krank.
sondernbut ratherV2 maintainedIch trinke keinen Kaffee, sondern ich trinke Tee.
💡 Key insight: These conjunctions are "zero position" — they don't count as position 1. They sit before the second main clause without affecting its word order.
🔴 Subordinating conjunctions — verb goes to THE END

weil, dass, obwohl, wenn, ob, als, seit, bis, während… — these send the conjugated verb to the very end of the subordinate clause. This is the most important A2 word order rule!

Main clause
Er bleibt zu Hause,
Conjunction
weil
Subject + rest
er
Verb → END
krank ist.
✓ weil (verb at end)
Er schläft, weil er müde ist.
He sleeps because he is tired.
✗ Wrong — verb not at end
Er schläft, weil er ist müde.
Common error: treating weil like denn!
✓ dass (verb at end)
Ich weiß, dass er morgen kommt.
I know that he is coming tomorrow.
✓ obwohl (verb at end)
Sie geht aus, obwohl sie krank ist.
She goes out even though she's sick.
⚠️ denn vs weil: Both mean "because" — but denn = coordinating (verb stays in pos. 2), weil = subordinating (verb goes to END). This is one of the most tested distinctions in German!
↩️ When the subordinate clause comes FIRST

If the subordinate clause precedes the main clause, the whole subordinate clause counts as position 1. The main clause then INVERTS — verb immediately after the comma.

Subordinate clause (= pos. 1)
Weil er krank ist,
Main verb (pos. 2!)
bleibt
Subject + rest
er zu Hause.
💡 The comma rule: After a subordinate clause, the main clause ALWAYS starts with the verb. Comma → verb. No exceptions!
✓ Sub clause first → inversion
Wenn ich Zeit habe, gehe ich ins Gym.
When I have time, I go to the gym.
✗ Wrong — forgot inversion
Wenn ich Zeit habe, ich gehe ins Gym.
After the comma, verb must come first!
⏰ Perfekt word order — bracket in main vs sub clause
Main clause (Hauptsatz)
Ich habe das Buch gelesen.
haben/sein in pos. 2 → Partizip II at END
Subordinate clause (Nebensatz)
…weil ich das Buch gelesen habe.
Partizip II → then haben/sein at VERY END
Sub-conjunction
weil
Subject
er
Object
das Buch
Partizip II + haben
gelesen hat.
💡 Sub clause Perfekt: Partizip II + haben/sein — both go to the end, with Partizip II BEFORE haben/sein.
🔗 Relative clauses — verb at the end

Relative clauses describe a noun. They work like subordinate clauses: verb at the end. They sit directly after the noun they describe.

Main clause
Das ist das Buch,
Relative pronoun
das
Object/subject
ich gestern
Verb → END
gekauft habe.
✓ Relative clause
Der Mann, der dort steht, ist mein Lehrer.
The man who is standing there is my teacher.
✓ With Perfekt
Das Film, den ich gesehen habe, war toll.
The film that I saw was great.
