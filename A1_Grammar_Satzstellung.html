⚡ The #1 rule: verb always in position 2

In every German main clause (Hauptsatz), the conjugated verb sits in the second position — no exceptions. This is the most important rule of German word order.

Position 1
Ich
Verb (pos. 2)
lerne
Rest
jeden Tag Deutsch.
Position 1
Heute
Verb (pos. 2)
lerne
Rest
ich Deutsch.

Notice: when "Heute" takes position 1, the verb stays in position 2 → the subject "ich" moves after the verb. This is called inversion.

💡 The golden rule: Only ONE element can fill position 1. Whatever it is, the conjugated verb immediately follows in position 2.
🔄 Inversion — subject moves after the verb

When anything other than the subject fills position 1 (a time word, a place, an object), the subject and verb swap. The verb stays in position 2, the subject moves to position 3.

✓ Normal order
Ich gehe morgen ins Kino.
I'm going to the cinema tomorrow.
✓ Inversion (time first)
Morgen gehe ich ins Kino.
Tomorrow I'm going to the cinema.
✗ Wrong — verb not in pos. 2
Morgen ich gehe ins Kino.
Subject forgot to swap!
✓ Object first (inversion)
Den Kaffee trinke ich morgens.
The coffee I drink in the mornings.
⚠️ Common mistake: After a time expression, students often forget to invert. "Morgen ich gehe..." → WRONG. The verb must come second!
🔒 The Satzklammer (sentence bracket)

Many German verbs have two parts. The conjugated part stays in position 2 — the second part (prefix, infinitive, Partizip II) goes to the very end. Everything else is "inside the bracket."

💡 Remember: Separable verbs split — prefix to the end. Modals keep the infinitive at the end. Perfekt keeps Partizip II at the end.
❓ Questions — two types, two rules

German has two question types, each with its own word order:

Yes/No question — verb FIRST
Kommst du morgen?
Are you coming tomorrow?
Hast du Hunger? · Ist er krank?
W-question — W-word first, verb second
Wann kommst du?
When are you coming?
