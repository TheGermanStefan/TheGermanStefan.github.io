🔦 Topicalization — moving elements to position 1 for emphasis

Any element (object, adverb, adverbial phrase) can be moved to position 1 (Vorfeld) to give it emphasis or create a narrative link. The verb always stays in position 2, so the subject shifts after it (inversion).

Neutral order (subject first)
Ich habe das Buch gestern gelesen.
Object topicalized (contrast/focus)
Das Buch habe ich gestern gelesen.
Adverb topicalized (time emphasis)
Gestern habe ich das Buch gelesen.
Prepositional phrase first (contrast)
Mit ihr möchte ich sprechen — nicht mit ihm.
💡 Topicalization principle: Whatever is in position 1 receives focus. Writers and speakers use this to guide attention, create contrast, or link back to previously mentioned information.
📌 Connective adverbs (deshalb, deswegen, trotzdem, daher, außerdem, dennoch) — these often start a sentence and CAUSE inversion: Er war müde. Trotzdem arbeitete er weiter. They fill position 1 → verb moves to position 2 → subject after verb.

💬 Indirect speech (Indirekte Rede) — Konjunktiv I

Indirect speech reports what someone said. In written/formal German, Konjunktiv I is used. Word order follows regular subordinate clause rules when introduced by "dass", OR keeps main clause order when "dass" is omitted.

With "dass" → verb at end
Er sagt, dass er morgen komme.
He says (that) he is coming tomorrow.
Without "dass" → V2 order
Er sagt, er komme morgen.
He says he is coming tomorrow. (no "that")
Indirect question (ob / W-word)
Er fragt, ob sie Zeit habe.
He asks whether she has time.
Indirect command (sollen)
Sie sagte, er solle warten.
She said he should wait.
💡 When Konj. I = same as indicative (e.g. ich komme), use Konj. II (käme) or würde-form instead to avoid ambiguity.
✨ Modal particles (Modalpartikeln) — position in the sentence

Modal particles (doch, ja, eigentlich, halt, eben, denn, wohl, mal) add tone and nuance. They always appear in the Mittelfeld — after the conjugated verb and before most other elements.
